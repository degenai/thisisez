How to read a card
Every specimen gets one card per (policy, universe): 250 simulated worlds, all final balances banked. The columns below are queries over those cards, at the specimen's best policy: WINS = universes (of 12) where it beats its own buy-and-hold in ≥50% of worlds · HODL = universes where even the parked version keeps ≥60% of capital (median) — the "could you hold this forever" gate · MEAN P = average probability of beating hold across all 12 universes · INSUR = P(beats hold) in the worlds where holding suffers most — the crash-insurance measure · CAL YIELD = the calibrated compiler's analytic prediction (yield ≈ 0.50·γ* − 0.84·dispersion − 0.02). Negative cal yield is normal and fine for a defensive demon — the trend-tax and friction terms push cold committees below zero; their value is WINS and INSUR, not income. Expect red numbers on excellent fortresses.

⑥ The engine ranking — the hunt verdict Census finding
A dispersion engine is an asset that generates large tradeable spreads against everything else while being hodlable itself (positive drift, no decay mechanics). The 42-symbol pool contained exactly one (GameStop). The question the 97-symbol census was built to answer: is GameStop the only one of its kind?
VERDICT: NO. The expansion found a family — MicroStrategy, Palantir, Tesla, Nvidia, uranium, oil, silver miners, Turkey. GameStop is still the apex (2.5× the runner-up's engine score), but the single-point-of-failure era is over. AMC tops the raw dispersion table and is disqualified on sight: −54%/yr drift. A corpse that moves is still a corpse.
