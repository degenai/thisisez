WHAT THIS IS — AND ISN'T. Yes: the underlying effect is the classic rebalancing bonus / diversification return, known to academic finance at least since Fernholz (1982) and to Shannon before that. We claim no new anomaly. Financial Demonology's contribution is instrumental: an exhaustive census of where the bonus is largest, a 12-universe tournament testing what survives structurally different market physics, and the finding that the payoff's true shape is insurance — most durable committees trade roughly free at the median and collect their edge in the tails. That is also the honest answer to "won't costs and taxes eat your median edge?" — the median was never the product; the tails are. (Why equal weight? Lesson one — Shannon's Demon, from zero
Take two assets that each go nowhere long-term but wander violently, and suppose they wander differently — when one is up the other is often down. Hold them 50/50 and rebalance on a schedule: trim whichever rose, buy whichever fell. Each trim sells high; each buy fills low; the discipline manufactures a positive compounding rate out of pure disagreement, even though neither asset earns anything on its own. Claude Shannon demonstrated this on a blackboard decades ago. It is not a paradox and not free money from nothing — it is payment for providing liquidity against your own book's noise. The catch, and the whole research program, is that the textbook version assumes no frictions, no taxes, and no trends. Everything below is about what survives contact with those.
The coin-flip game. One asset flips a fair coin every trading day: heads +5.00%, tails −4.76% — sized so one head and one tail exactly cancel (1.05 × 0.95238 = 1.0000). The coin goes nowhere: parked, its median outcome after any horizon is exactly what you put in. Now put $1,000 on the table, half in the coin and half in cash, and rebalance back to 50/50 after every flip:

Sixty cents per head-tail pair sounds like nothing — until you run it at Atlas length: 1,530 trading days, the same ~6-year window every card on this page uses, and the sixty cents compounds to +58%, manufactured from pure noise. Heads and tails in any order: every head gets trimmed, every tail gets bought, so direction never matters — the demon eats the wander itself. Meanwhile the holder's average outcome looks respectable (dragged up by rare lucky streaks — that flattering mean is exactly what volatility does to parked books), but his median world ends where it started. And notice the thesis in miniature: the coin was hodlable — its median path goes nowhere rather than to zero — yet every dollar of profit came from the trades. The extra money is paid by the counterparties who sell the dips and buy the rips that the rebalancer takes the other side of. How much can this yield in general? The demon lives in the spread between assets, never in an asset. Excess growth rate of a pair ≈ ½ · var(return spread) · 252 — annualized pairwise demon yield; the doctrine calls this γ (gamma). This one scalar is the heat map the whole apparatus is built around.
Lesson two — the operator, not the rentier
An ETF is congealed multiplicity — a hundred firms in one handle. The rentier re-atomizes it: relates to the bundle as a single object to hold or flee, and reaches for a 200-day moving average as their only defense. The operator keeps it as one node in an ensemble and works the edges between nodes. Composability is the whole game — and it is also a class position. You are not asked to predict anything; you are asked to run a machine whose fuel is the volatility everyone else is fleeing.

Lesson four — the aggression dial Seven verified demons
Demons rank by order — how many sleeves sit on the committee (1st order = a pair, 2nd = a triad, and so on). The census — the 97-symbol, ~42-million-world sweep that populated the Atlas — taught us what order actually is: the aggression dial. Low order concentrates the engine — feast, offense, fat yield, single points of failure. High order widens the shell — defense, insurance, survival everywhere, thin yield. The price per click is mathematical: equal-weight committees dilute each pairwise edge by 1/k². Lesson five — insurance, precisely Real per-world data (simulated vault)
Every card carries an insurance number, and it's the most misread metric on the shelf, so here is the exact definition: take all 250 simulated worlds, sort them by how the parked (buy-and-hold) version did, and look only at the worst quartile — the disasters. Insurance = the share of those disaster-worlds where working the book beat parking it. The mechanism is Law I, Prime the Spring (stated in full with the seven laws below): rebalancing into drawdowns keeps buying low-basis lots all the way down, so in the worlds where holding gets wrecked, the harvester owns more shares at the bottom and recovers on a spring. The cost is the mirror image: in melt-up worlds the discipline keeps trimming the winner and surrenders upside. So a true insurance organ shows a signature shape: green on the left (paid in disasters), red on the right (premium paid in booms).
The cleanest way to see it is a controlled experiment the vault happened to contain: two committees that are statistically identical on every headline number — both turn $1,000 into ~$2,250 at the median of the same 250 worlds, both beat parking in 70% of them — but one carries insurance and one doesn't. Gold + broad commodities (insurance 0.85) vs US small caps + dividend stocks (insurance 0.50). Identical returns, identical win rate; the only difference is where the wins live. Toggle between them and watch the shaded left edge — the disasters — flip from a green wall to coin flips. That difference is the entire insurance column. Then switch to the advanced pair: the Lab-7 shows a real premium being charged — read the moonshot-zone label: in the worlds where holding moons hardest, the harvester surrenders upside, and that surrender is what buys its 0.92 disaster coverage — while the five-point fortress (base metals / UK / Brazil / gold miners / Turkey — a different book from the 7-sleeve TQQQ fortress) shows coverage with no visible premium at all.

Read a card's insurance number like an adjuster, asking two questions. One: coverage. 0.50 = coin flip (no insurance, whatever the median says); 0.75+ = real coverage, three of four disasters cushioned. Two: the premium — check the annotated median world above. The matched pair prices the concept: identical $2,250 medians, identical 70% win rates, and the insured book still wins 85% of disasters with a +6% cushion — in this corner of the market, the coverage came free. It usually doesn't: coverage normally costs something somewhere — the Lab-7 charges its premium in the moonshot zone (surrendered melt-up upside; read the zone label when you toggle to it) in exchange for 0.92 disaster coverage. Coverage minus premium is the number that matters, and finding committees where it's large and positive is most of what the census hunt was for.
The seven laws Built & tested
I · Prime the Spring
Rebalance INTO drawdowns
Each buy of a crashing sleeve is a low-basis, high-share lot. The discipline loads the spring under tension; the reversion releases it. Drawdown isn't the enemy — it's the raw material.
II · The Bite
Trim rich → buy poor, $1 units
Sell one executable dollar-unit from the most-over-target sleeve, buy it into the most-under. Human-runnable quanta, no fractional-penny fantasy.
III · The Plateau
Bite size is a drawdown & tax lever, not a returns lever
1%–5% of book all return the same. Size controls drawdown and taxable events — and it's cadence-conditional: every-2-days @ 500bps ≈ daily @ 200bps.
IV · The Free Demon
Contributions deploy dispersion-first
Route each drip to the most-underweight sleeve — never pro-rata, never selling. Free rebalancing, zero taxable events. But only where there's real dispersion; on a flat book the cash waits.
V · The Tax Knob
Widen the band, not the returns
5% → 10% completion band = identical returns, ~4× fewer taxable events. The band is a tax dial.
VI · Demon Routing
Advisory only
Route through the highest spread-variance pair when several qualify. Under resampling it's a coin flip (50.4%). Tiebreaker, never an oracle.
VII · The Trend Gate
OFF
The 200-day trend filter is a passive holder's tool. It cost ~2% of terminal wealth in every window to shave the productive drawdown the harvest is paid to take. Anti-spring. Dropped.
The Tournament of Power — twelve universes Built · ~42M worlds simulated
Bootstrap worlds from one dataset are all children of one reality — the same market physics reshuffled. A demon that survives them has survived one universe's statistics. Real robustness means surviving structurally different physics. So every specimen runs across twelve universe-types, 250 worlds each. A demon that fails a universe is erased from it. The Atlas method — how the judging works The vault is public doctrine
Early on we had a "judge" — a script that ranked demons and kept summary verdicts. We killed it. Its replacement is the Atlas: an append-only vault of raw per-world outcomes. Every cell = one (committee, policy, universe) triple, 250 simulated worlds, every final balance written to disk the moment it exists. Judging is a query, run at read time — change your mind about the metric and you re-read, never re-simulate. Crashes cost nothing (the vault survives; the run resumes). Deepening is additive. The current vault: 168,575 cells across a 97-symbol pool — roughly 42 million simulated worlds. On top of it sit three instruments:
The Compiler
Committee yield compiles from pairs
Fernholz's stochastic portfolio theory: a committee's harvest is the weighted sum of its edges' spread variances. Calibrated against 861 measured pairs: yield ≈ 0.50·γ* − 0.84·dispersion − 0.02 (R² = 0.93; γ* = the committee's compiled gamma from Lesson One). Reading note: negative calibrated yield is normal for defensive demons — the trend-tax and ~2%/yr friction terms push cold committees below zero; their value is universes won and insurance, not income. Only aggressive, engine-led demons print meaningfully positive. The dispersion term is the trend tax — buy-and-hold's momentum bonus that raw theory can't see. Screens millions of committees analytically; survival still has to be simulated.
The Hodl Gate
A column, not a filter
Every card records whether the committee's held version survives (median hold ≥ 60% of capital in every universe). Un-hodlable "winners" — vol products, leveraged decay pairs — beat holding only because holding them is a funeral. Zero exceptions found so far; still a law-candidate, not a law.
The Ablation Protocol
Higher orders must earn their order
Every serious committee gets leave-one-out testing against all its subsets and banked edges. If performance survives every ablation except minus-X, the committee was just X-with-bodyguards. The Sculptor runs this backward: start from the whole pool, carve greedily down, record the champion at every order.
The census also answered a single-asset question worth naming here: a dispersion engine is an asset that generates large tradeable spreads against everything else while remaining hodlable itself — loud but alive. The original 42-symbol pool contained exactly one (GameStop). Whether it was the only one of its kind is the question the 97-symbol expansion was built to answer; the verdict, and the full engine ranking, live on the Atlas.
Cautionary ward, learned the expensive way: metrics produce artifacts. An inverse-leveraged pair once topped the yield leaderboard at +34%/yr — on a +0.2% CAGR (rebalancing "beat" two instruments grinding to zero). A 3× fund plus its own index topped the CAGR board — a regime trophy, not a strategy. And the beloved forum portfolio HFEA (55/45 UPRO/TMF, rebalanced quarterly)? Sweeping all 99 ratios found the best one was 99/1 — as little TMF as possible. The doctrine's own rebalancing was the murder weapon, faithfully marrying the winner to an asset that fell 89% from its 2020 peak to its 2023 trough. Exhaustive search finds coincidences by construction; the tournament and the gates exist to kill them.
The pentagram — the construction principle 12/12 universes · 12/12 hodl
Committees built for maximum compiled yield verify with regime holes. But a committee where every one of its ten pairwise edges is independently elite — each surviving at least 11 of 12 universes, five of them perfect at 12/12+12/12 — composed into the first committee-level card to sweep all twelve universes: five real, dividend-paying, boring assets, no leverage, no memes. Drawn with its chords, the topology is literally our sigil. Yield compiles from pairs (provable); survival doesn't compile — but all-edges-elite is the closest thing to a survival construction rule we've found. The 97-symbol census has since surfaced seven pentagrams whose every edge is perfect — see the Atlas for the live leaderboard.

Methodology — the epistemics
  • Ledger over theory. The broker's confirmed fills outrank every backtest and every doc, including this one. Reconcile to 6 decimals.
  • Parity gates. Before any comparison, the unchanged code path is re-run and hash-compared byte-for-byte. Every feature ships inert-by-default. The fast vectorized engine is only trusted where it matches the reference engine to tolerance — where it doesn't, the slow engine stays canonical.
  • The tournament as judge. Findings must survive twelve universes, not one leaderboard. In-sample wins are hypotheses, not results.
  • Selection-bias honesty. Exhaustive search over thousands of demons produces winners by construction. Log what was dropped; never quote a top row as doctrine.
  • Append-only results. Raw outcomes are written once and never edited; conclusions are queries over the vault. Anyone can re-ask a different question of the same data.
  • The primitive stack. Stdlib Python, numpy where parity-gated, vanilla HTML/JS. Libraries, not frameworks. Don't hold what everyone can farm.
