Lesson zero — why this exists, and why it is free
Do the arithmetic of renting your returns. If your money grows through someone else's product — the 1% advisor wrap, the layered fund fees, the structured anything — the lessor earns your return plus your rent, while holding the same market you do. A 1% annual fee sounds like nothing and compounds to roughly a third of your terminal wealth over a 40-year working life, at any market return (0.99⁴⁰ ≈ 0.67 — the fee is charged on assets, not gains). By construction, the renter can never catch the lessor. Not talent. Arithmetic. You cannot rent a strategy from the owning class and come out ahead of it. The only move is owning the process.
So the field is free — doctrine, data, games, code, all of it. The apparatus of real returns — simulators, stress tournaments, the honest pricing of insurance — is exactly what the fee machine keeps behind institutional walls, and Financial Demonology publishes it. Better: the premium this field studies is the rare market payment that structurally favors the small — it lives in corners too thin for big capital, needs no scale, and pays a wage for discipline rather than rent for access. Being a small operator is the moat, not the handicap. Nothing on this page promises riches (read the banners; they mean it). It promises ownership — of your process, your tools, and the wage your own patience earns. Robin Hood, with receipts.
Lesson one — Shannon's Demon, from zero
Take two assets that each go nowhere long-term but wander violently, and suppose they wander differently — when one is up the other is often down. Hold them 50/50 and rebalance on a schedule: trim whichever rose, buy whichever fell. Each trim sells high; each buy fills low; the discipline manufactures a positive compounding rate out of pure disagreement, even though neither asset earns anything on its own. Claude Shannon demonstrated this on a blackboard decades ago. It is not a paradox and not free money from nothing — it is payment for providing liquidity against your own book's noise. The catch, and the whole research program, is that the textbook version assumes no frictions, no taxes, and no trends. Everything below is about what survives contact with those.
The coin-flip game. One asset flips a fair coin every trading day: heads +5.00%, tails −4.76% — sized so one head and one tail exactly cancel (1.05 × 0.95238 = 1.0000). The coin goes nowhere: parked, its median outcome after any horizon is exactly what you put in. Now put $1,000 on the table, half in the coin and half in cash, and rebalance back to 50/50 after every flip:

Two facts about the game you just played. Why 50/50? Because for this coin it is a theorem, not a guess: a symmetric multiplicative coin owes the player exactly half — any other split lowers the median world, though the hill is forgivingly flat from ~34% to ~66%, and under trading costs the shelf holds: any sane rebalance band recovers the friction tax (Laws III and V, alive even in the toy). And why a ±5% coin? Pure honesty about what size buys: the coin's wildness is the fuel. Per 1,530-flip window, the median world pays +18% on a ±3% coin, +58% on the ±5% coin above, and +468% on a ±10% coin (exact, before costs; ~+440% after, at 30,000 simulated worlds). There is no optimum — louder is strictly better in the toy, because the toy's coin is guaranteed safe to hold at any volume. Real assets are not: crank a real asset's wildness and it usually stops surviving. An asset that is BOTH loud AND alive is the rarest thing in the market — which is exactly what the census hunts, and why the engine ranking on the Atlas matters.
The demon lives in the spread between assets, never in an asset. Excess growth rate of a pair ≈ ½ · var(return spread) · 252 — annualized pairwise demon yield; the doctrine calls this γ (gamma). This one scalar is the heat map the whole apparatus is built around.
Lesson two — the operator, not the rentier
An ETF is congealed multiplicity — a hundred firms in one handle. The rentier re-atomizes it: relates to the bundle as a single object to hold or flee, and reaches for a 200-day moving average as their only defense. The operator keeps it as one node in an ensemble and works the edges between nodes. Composability is the whole game — and it is also a class position. You are not asked to predict anything; you are asked to run a machine whose fuel is the volatility everyone else is fleeing.

Lesson five — insurance, precisely Real per-world data (simulated vault)
Every card carries an insurance number, and it's the most misread metric on the shelf, so here is the exact definition: take all 250 simulated worlds, sort them by how the parked (buy-and-hold) version did, and look only at the worst quartile — the disasters. Insurance = the share of those disaster-worlds where working the book beat parking it. The mechanism is Law I, Prime the Spring (stated in full with the seven laws below): rebalancing into drawdowns keeps buying low-basis lots all the way down, so in the worlds where holding gets wrecked, the harvester owns more shares at the bottom and recovers on a spring. The cost is the mirror image: in melt-up worlds the discipline keeps trimming the winner and surrenders upside. So a true insurance organ shows a signature shape: green on the left (paid in disasters), red on the right (premium paid in booms).
The cleanest way to see it is a controlled experiment the vault happened to contain: two committees that are statistically identical on every headline number — both turn $1,000 into ~$2,250 at the median of the same 250 worlds, both beat parking in 70% of them — but one carries insurance and one doesn't. Gold + broad commodities (insurance 0.85) vs US small caps + dividend stocks (insurance 0.50). Identical returns, identical win rate; the only difference is where the wins live. Toggle between them and watch the shaded left edge — the disasters — flip from a green wall to coin flips. That difference is the entire insurance column. Then switch to the advanced pair: the Lab-7 shows a real premium being charged — read the moonshot-zone label: in the worlds where holding moons hardest, the harvester surrenders upside, and that surrender is what buys its 0.92 disaster coverage — while the five-point fortress (base metals / UK / Brazil / gold miners / Turkey — a different book from the 7-sleeve TQQQ fortress) shows coverage with no visible premium at all.

The objection you should be raising: "isn't this automatic? In worlds where parking did badly, doesn't trading look better by sheer arithmetic?" No — and the proof is one click away: toggle to the UNINSURED book. Same 250 worlds, same sorting, same arithmetic — and its disaster zone collapses to a coin flip (+0% median). If the green wall came free with the sorting, the uninsured book would have one too. It doesn't. The sorting can't manufacture coverage; only the book's structure — legs that keep disagreeing while everything falls — can. Killing exactly this objection is why the matched pair is the exhibit.
Read a card's insurance number like an adjuster, asking two questions. One: coverage. 0.50 = coin flip (no insurance, whatever the median says); 0.75+ = real coverage, three of four disasters cushioned. Two: the premium — check the annotated median world above. The matched pair prices the concept: identical $2,250 medians, identical 70% win rates, and the insured book still wins 85% of disasters with a +6% cushion — in this corner of the market, the coverage came free. It usually doesn't: coverage normally costs something somewhere — the Lab-7 charges its premium in the moonshot zone (surrendered melt-up upside; read the zone label when you toggle to it) in exchange for 0.92 disaster coverage. Coverage minus premium is the number that matters, and finding committees where it's large and positive is most of what the census hunt was for.
The seven laws Built & tested
I · Prime the Spring
Rebalance INTO drawdowns
Each buy of a crashing sleeve is a low-basis, high-share lot. The discipline loads the spring under tension; the reversion releases it. Drawdown isn't the enemy — it's the raw material.
II · The Bite
Trim rich → buy poor, $1 units
Sell one executable dollar-unit from the most-over-target sleeve, buy it into the most-under. Human-runnable quanta, no fractional-penny fantasy.
III · The Plateau
Bite size is a drawdown & tax lever, not a returns lever
1%–5% of book all return the same. Size controls drawdown and taxable events — and it's cadence-conditional: every-2-days @ 500bps ≈ daily @ 200bps.
IV · The Free Demon
Contributions deploy dispersion-first
Route each drip to the most-underweight sleeve — never pro-rata, never selling. Free rebalancing, zero taxable events. But only where there's real dispersion; on a flat book the cash waits.
V · The Tax Knob
Widen the band, not the returns
5% → 10% completion band = identical returns, ~4× fewer taxable events. The band is a tax dial.
VI · Demon Routing
Advisory only
Route through the highest spread-variance pair when several qualify. Under resampling it's a coin flip (50.4%). Tiebreaker, never an oracle.
VII · The Trend Gate
OFF
The 200-day trend filter is a passive holder's tool. It cost ~2% of terminal wealth in every window to shave the productive drawdown the harvest is paid to take. Anti-spring. Dropped.

The tax man — and the lever nobody told you about Engine v3.2, audited
Everything above this line is pre-tax, and for a long time so was every comparison we ran — which, it turns out, was a rigged fight. We taxed the trader on every gain they realized and then compared them to a buy-and-hold twin we never taxed at all. Of course the trader looked bad. That isn't a stress test, it's a stacked deck.

So we built the tax code properly: per-lot ledgers, the wash-sale rule (§1091 — 30 days either side, disallowed loss shifted into the replacement lot's basis, holding period tacked), the IRS anniversary rule (long-term means held more than a year — a sale on the anniversary is still short-term), loss carryovers that keep their character, state tax, and amended returns when a later purchase washes a loss you already filed. It is checked line-by-line against an independently written accountant — a second implementation, built from the IRS publications rather than from our engine — and the two must agree to the penny on every year's bill, or the build fails. Then we made the twin sell too, on the same day, under the same code. Both sides exit. Both sides pay. Then you ask who won.
Here is what fell out, and it is not what we expected. The line marked FIFO — "first in, first out" — is the setting your broker uses by default, unless you tell it otherwise. The line marked HIFO is the same demon, the same worlds, the same trades, changing one thing only: which tax lots the seller points at.

⚠️ THESE NUMBERS ARE UNDER RECONSTRUCTION. READ THIS BEFORE YOU READ THE TABLE.

We sent an independent auditor to check our tax engine against the Internal Revenue Code — not against our own test suite — and it found three separate errors. What matters is not that there were three. It is that all three push the same direction: every one of them inflates the FIFO-vs-HIFO gap that is the headline of this section.

Three independent errors all flattering the same conclusion is the shape of motivated reasoning, even when nobody was motivated. So we are saying it here, at the top, in red, instead of in a footnote.

The errors: (1) we never modeled the $3,000/yr capital-loss deduction (§1211(b)) — measured at 6.5–9.8% of lifetime tax on hot books, and 1.5× worse for FIFO than HIFO; (2) the low end of every range below assumes an unused capital loss is worth zero — but under the actual law it's usable at your ordinary rate, so the truth sits at the TOP of each range, not between; (3) two of our assets aren't taxed the way we taxed them at all (below). Corrected numbers are being rebuilt. Until then, read the top of each range and treat the gap as smaller than shown.

Read that table again, because it is the most important thing on this page. On the default setting, six years of disciplined harvesting underperforms doing nothing. Change nothing but which shares you point at when you sell, and the same strategy beats the market — and pays half the tax. The lot-selection default is the difference between a strategy and a hobby, and the default is the losing arm.

And here is the part we expected to be bad news, got wrong once, and then measured properly. Robinhood caps a specified-lot sale at 30 lots, and a six-year sleeve holds hundreds — so we assumed the placeable lever would be a shadow of the simulated one. We even published that. It's false. Under the cap, the tax-cost rule beats FIFO in 522 of 576 cells (+7.8% median) and gives up 0.02% against the uncapped version. (We first published this as "+11.1% in 567 cells." That was computed on the bottom of the range — the bound our own red box above tells you not to read, because it values an unused capital loss at zero when the law says it is worth your ordinary rate. Quoting our headline off the bound we told you to distrust is exactly the error we keep making. +7.8% is the honest number.) HIFO under the cap gives up the same 0.02%.

Our mistake is worth showing, because it's a good one: we had measured how many lots a sale touches (a median of 21 for HIFO, sometimes 500 — so 94% of its volume does blow past the cap) and reported that as if it were money. It isn't. The first 30 lots carry essentially all of the tax benefit; the long tail of lots is worth almost nothing. The cap binds constantly and costs you nearly zero. We measured the wrong proxy and published it — this is the correction.
Why it works
Your basis is a choice
A rebalancer buys the same ticker over and over, so a sleeve accumulates dozens of lots at different prices. When you trim, the tax you owe depends entirely on which of those purchases you call the one you're selling. Sell the cheap old shares (FIFO) and you hand the IRS a giant gain. Sell the expensive recent ones (HIFO) and you realize little — or a loss. Same shares. Same money. Different bill.
The catch that wasn't
The 30-lot cap is free
Brokers cap a specified-lot sale (Robinhood: 30 lots per order) and a six-year sleeve holds hundreds, so we expected the cap to gut the strategy. It costs 0.02%. The cap binds constantly — on a hot book a HIFO sale wants a median of 21 lots and sometimes 500 — but the first 30 lots carry essentially all the tax benefit. The tail is worthless. On a cold book a sale touches ~2 lots and the cap never even engages. You can pull this lever by hand, in the app, and lose nothing.
The honest caveat
We under-tax the holder
Our price data is total-return — dividends are baked in as growth — so our simulated holder defers tax on everything for six years. A real holder pays tax on dividends every year, sale or no sale. That is the one bill deferral can't dodge, and we aren't charging it. So this comparison is biased against the trader and the real FIFO gap is narrower than shown. We flag it loudly precisely because it favors our own result. Fixing it needs a dividend-split panel owed.
Assets we taxed wrong
Gold isn't stock
SLV and GLDM are bullion trusts. Their long-term gains are collectibles (§1(h)(4)) — taxed up to 28%, not 15%. For a mid-bracket investor that means short-term and long-term cost exactly the same: holding gold a year buys you nothing, and every lot rule that prefers long-term gains is ranking gold backwards. Meanwhile USO is a §1256 commodity pool — marked to market annually whether you sell or not, character fixed 60/40, no holding-period clock, and §1256(f)(5) says the wash-sale rule expressly does not apply to it. We gave these funds lots, holding periods and wash sales. They have none. Rebuilding
Our auditors disagreed
And the second one was right
One auditor told us DBB was a §1256 mark-to-market fund. We believed it and published it here. A second auditor pulled DBB's actual prospectus: "the Fund does not invest in contracts subject to Section 1256." Its metals trade on the London Metal Exchange, which isn't a qualified board of exchange. The first auditor was wrong and we amplified it. DBB is still mis-modeled — it's a partnership that hands you phantom income you're taxed on without receiving — but for a completely different reason, and the "fix" we were about to ship would itself have been wrong. Adversarial review only works if you keep doing it after it agrees with you.
The bill arrives sooner than we said
Estimated taxes
We settle the tax bill on December 31. The IRS wants it in four installments (§6654 — April, June, September, January), with a penalty for underpaying. Our December-31 model hands the portfolio an interest-free loan of up to nine months of its own tax bill, every year, and never charges the penalty. We called that "conservative" in our own code comments. It is the opposite — it flatters every taxed result on this page, including ours.
Now the part that should make you angry. Specific-lot identification is not a secret, an exploit, or a loophole. It is a checkbox. It has been legal and standard for decades — and it is routine for anyone whose money is managed by a professional, because their advisor's software does it automatically on every sale. Everyone else is silently defaulted into FIFO: the arm that, on our numbers, turns a working strategy into a losing one.

That is worth being precise about, because it is the whole thesis of this site in miniature. This is not alpha we discovered. It is alpha that was always there, allocated by who has an accountant. It is the rent theorem with a receipt: the people you rent returns from are not merely collecting your fee — they are playing a different tax game with the same trades. The lever costs nothing. It is in the menu. Nobody sells it to you because nobody makes money selling you something free.

The optimizer that doesn't optimize Quoted from their own disclosures
We built a third rule to test — one that sorts lots by tax character: all the short-term losses first, then the long-term losses, then the long-term gains, and finally the short-term gains. We expected it to win, and when it lost we assumed we'd written it badly.

Then we went and read the brokers' disclosures, and found we had reinvented theirs — exactly.

Schwab's Tax Lot Optimizer™ selects lots in this order: greatest short-term loss first, down to the least; then greatest long-term loss down to the least; then the zero-gain lots; then the least long-term gain up to the greatest; and finally the least short-term gain up to the greatest.

Read that twice and notice what is not in it: a tax rate. Nowhere does the rule multiply anything by what you'd actually owe. It sorts lots into six buckets by character, and only compares sizes inside a bucket. Vanguard's MinTax is the same algorithm, and Vanguard publishes the identical ordering. Fidelity ships this one too, as "Tax-Sensitive Short-Term."

Which produces a genuinely absurd behavior, and to their credit, Vanguard publishes it themselves. Because every long-term gain is ranked ahead of every short-term gain, the method will hand you a $100,000 long-term gain instead of a $10 short-term gain — since long-term gains are taxed at a nicer rate. You save on the rate and pay a fortune on the bill. Vanguard's own documentation says it plainly: "The shares with the most favorable tax rate might not be the ones with the lowest gain."

In a buy-and-hold account this rarely bites. In a book that has been rebalancing for six years it bites constantly, because the long-term lots are the oldest ones — which means the cheapest ones — which means the biggest gains. The rule reaches straight for them. That is why it lost.

Sorting by tax rate is not the same as sorting by tax owed. Only one major broker's standing method actually computes what you'd owe — Fidelity's "Tax-Sensitive," which ranks lots by tax cost per share. The rule in the table above is of that kind (ours uses our own rate assumptions, not Fidelity's hardcoded 35%/15%, so it is a cousin and not a clone). The others are named "optimizer" and "MinTax" and they are neither.

Three things we learned the hard way Operational
Robinhood
FIFO or nothing
There is no setting to make Robinhood default to HIFO or a tax-cost rule — FIFO is the only standing method it has. Specific lots exist per order only (30 max, sells only, not on dollar-amount or stop orders). So the lever is real, and it must be pulled on every single sale, forever, by hand or by API. The default is not a suggestion; it's the floor you fall to every time you don't act.
Harvesting inside a rebalancer
The demon is the wash
A wash sale disallows your loss if you rebuy within 30 days — and a rebalancer's next trade IS the rebuy. So "harvest the loss" strategies quietly cancel themselves. The academic work prices harvesting by avoiding the wash (park in cash 30 days); the robo-advisors avoid it by buying a different ETF. Nobody has published what happens when you simply eat it — which is what an automatic rebalancer does by construction. It can't design the wash out; the wash is its own next buy. Every number on this page already has eaten it. We're now running the counterfactual to price exactly what it costs. Running
The trap that eats the loss forever
Don't share a ticker with your IRA
A wash in a taxable account only defers the loss — it moves into the replacement's basis. But if the replacement is bought inside an IRA or 401(k), the loss is destroyed permanently: no basis adjustment, ever (Rev. Rul. 2008-5). If your retirement account holds the same ticker your taxable account is harvesting, you are not being clever — you are setting money on fire. Keep the ticker sets disjoint.

Read the top-right cell again, because it is the whole section. It is not a low rate, a deduction, a credit, or a deferral. Under §1(h), long-term capital gain that lands inside the 0% bracket is taxed at zero percent. It has been in the code for years. It is not a loophole — there is nothing to close, nothing to disclose, nothing to be clever about. It is the plain rate on the plain page. And essentially nobody who qualifies for it knows it exists, because nobody has ever been paid to tell them.
Now the move that falls out of it, and it is the best thing in this entire research campaign.

The wash-sale rule — §1091, the rule that has haunted every paragraph of the tax section above, the one that makes loss harvesting inside a rebalancer cancel itself — contains a word we read a hundred times before we read it. It disallows a loss. Only a loss. It says nothing whatsoever about gains.

So the Broke Boy does not harvest losses. He harvests gains. He sells a long-term winner, pays zero federal tax on the gain, and rebuys the identical share the same minute — same ticker, no 30-day wait, no substantially-identical dance, no cash drag, because there is no rule to trip. He is out of the market for the length of one round trip. What he gets back is the same position with a higher cost basis: every dollar of gain he just washed through the 0% bracket is a dollar that can never be taxed again, in any future year, at any future rate, even after his income rises and the door closes behind him.
Free, permanent, repeatable. The loss harvester's basis step-down is a loan from the IRS that must be repaid on sale. The gain harvester's basis step-up is not a loan. It is a purchase of future tax immunity, at a price of zero. Run it every year until your income grows out of the bracket, and you carry the accumulated step-ups with you into the Citizen's world forever. This is the only thing on this page that is strictly free.
And then the punchline lands on the section above, which we published in good faith and which is wrong for him. FIFO — first in, first out, the "losing arm," the silent default we spent a whole section attacking, the one Robinhood forces on you with no way to turn it off — may be his optimal method. FIFO sells the oldest lots. The oldest lots are the ones with the longest holding periods. Long holding periods mean long-term character. And long-term character is precisely the thing that, in his body, is free. The method that hands the Citizen a giant taxable gain hands the Broke Boy a giant untaxable one — and steps his basis up for nothing while it does.

The default is not a trap for everyone. It is a trap for the people the trap was described by.

THE CRUELTY, AND IT IS STRUCTURAL. Read the threshold one more time — it is taxable income, and capital gains are part of taxable income. The gain counts against its own ceiling. Which means the 0% bracket is not a reward for being a patient small investor. It is a consequence of being poor, and it closes the instant you stop being poor — including if your investing is what stops it.

Having enough income to invest is what disqualifies you from the best tax treatment of investing. The people with the most capital are structurally locked out of the 0% bracket by the very income that gave them the capital. The people standing inside it usually have nothing to put in it. It is a door that only opens for people with no furniture — and it is a self-liquidating advantage: succeed loudly enough and you evict yourself.

Note how the last row of the table plays the same trick in reverse. Even the consolation prize is means-tested upward: $3,000 of capital loss shelters ~$1,200 for the Citizen and ~$510 for the Broke Boy, because it offsets ordinary income at your own rate. Losses, like everything else in the code, are worth the most to the person who can most afford to eat them.
The one place his superpower is worthless — and why it inverts the advice
Look back at the gold row. The Broke Boy pays $169.90 on a long-term gold gain — the lowest gold tax bill of any of the three, less than half the Citizen's. And it is a catastrophe for him, in a way it is not for the Citizen.

Because bullion trusts are collectibles under §1(h)(4) (the "Gold isn't stock" card above), and collectibles get no long-term discount at all — you pay your ordinary rate, capped at 28%. For the Citizen that cap is a penalty: 28% instead of 15%, roughly 1.5× worse than a stock. For the Broke Boy the penalty is not 1.5×, it is infinite in ratio: his alternative was zero. Gold is the one asset in the world where his single best advantage — the only free thing he owns — is worth exactly nothing. His long-term gold gain costs him the same as a short-term stock gain, to the penny. The holding-period clock, the entire year of patience, buys him nothing on metal.

Absolute rate is the wrong lens. What matters is the rate relative to your own best alternative — and by that measure, the man who pays the least tax on gold is the man most punished for holding it in a taxable account. So his asset location does not shade the standard advice. It inverts it.

Why nobody told you — and it isn't a conspiracy
There is a temptation here to reach for a villain, and it is worth resisting, because the truth is duller and much more damning.

SO WHICH HOST ARE YOU? IT IS ONE SUBTRACTION.

You do not need software, an advisor, or this website. You need one number off your own tax return, and it is the same number in both places:

1. Find TAXABLE INCOME — Form 1040, line 15. If you don't have the return, pull a free Return Transcript or Account Transcript at irs.gov/transcripts (it takes about ninety seconds and no money) and read the line labeled TAXABLE INCOME. This is not your salary and not your gross — it is what is left after your deductions, and it is usually far lower than people expect. That is the entire trick: most people who are in the 0% bracket have no idea, because they are looking at the wrong line.

2. Subtract it from the 0% long-term ceiling: $49,450 single, roughly double that married filing jointly (2026 — the figure is indexed and moves every year; head of household sits in between; look up the current one, do not trust a website).

3. Read the answer. Negative → you are the Citizen; go back and re-read the lot-selection section, it was written for you. Positive → that number is your headroom: the dollars of long-term gain you can realize this calendar year at a federal rate of zero. It expires December 31 and does not carry forward. Unused headroom is simply gone.

The author's own transcript: $11,620 taxable income. $49,450 − $11,620 = $37,830 of headroom. Thirty-seven thousand dollars of long-term gain he can realize this year, and rebuy the same minute, for a federal tax of zero.

And it is a bracket, not a cliff: if you overshoot, only the excess is taxed (at 15%) — the part underneath stays free. There is no penalty for aiming at the ceiling and missing.

The frown — concavity, and the cancellation we measured Mechanism + our own numbers
Draw one picture in your head and most of this page falls out of it. Take any strategy and plot its profit against the size of a market move — small moves in the middle, huge moves at the edges. The shape of that curve is the strategy's soul. Buy-and-hold is a straight line: asset doubles, you make 2×; asset halves, you lose half. Trend-following is a smile — the curve bends up at both ends: it bleeds a little in chop (that's its premium paid) and wins disproportionately in monster moves; Fung & Hsieh (2001) formalized it as a "lookback straddle." A rebalancer is a frown — the curve bends down at both ends. The formal name is negative convexity (Perold & Sharpe, 1988), and here is why it's unavoidable: a rebalancer, by construction, sells whatever's rising and buys whatever's falling. In an oscillating market that's buy-low-sell-high on repeat — every wiggle a small payday; that's the happy middle of the frown, the harvest this whole page is about. But feed it one huge one-way move and both of its reflexes betray it at once: it trims the winner all the way up (surrendering the upside) and buys the loser all the way down (averaging into the grave). Our GME/AMC autopsy is the frown's left edge in its purest form: six years of dutifully selling the thing that went up 20× to buy the thing that went to zero — trade-added −73%. The damage from a trend grows faster than the trend. That accelerating hurt at the extremes IS the concavity. An insurance company has exactly this payoff shape — premiums every calm month, catastrophe payouts in the hurricane — which is why quants say the rebalancer "sells volatility." (Shape only: it is like a short straddle, never exactly one — no strike, no expiry, no cash premium, no unbounded tail. We once published the word "exactly" and retracted it.) And the deepest one-liner: rebalanced wealth grows like the geometric mean of its legs, buy-and-hold like the arithmetic mean, and the geometric mean is always dragged down by spread between outcomes. The frown is the AM–GM inequality wearing a P&L.
THE CANCELLATION (Perold & Sharpe): a frown plus a smile is a straight line. The rebalancer sells convexity; the trend-follower buys it; buyer + seller = buy-and-hold. So if you ever try to fully hedge a rebalancer's crash-fragility with a trend overlay, you will pay two sets of trading costs to own… buy-and-hold. The mechanism and its antidote cancel exactly, and the fee meter runs the whole time.
AND WE MEASURED IT — the consequence almost no retail research ever checks. When we built tail-guards for the rebalancer, every variant that (in effect) hedged the frown quietly became an expensive momentum clone: the purchase-cap rule ran R² = 0.96 against a plain 12-1 momentum sleeve and cost −16.2%/yr; relative-strength ran R² = 0.94 at −16.9%/yr; a naive stop-loss cost −6.77%/yr. None of them banned feeding the corpse — they banned feeding the laggard, and in a book with one big winner the laggard is a healthy asset you should be buying. The one rule that survived is truncation, not hedging: stop buying a member once it sits more than 80% below its own running high, keep holding it, resume if it recovers. Kill one: γ* — the harvest this whole page is named for — is captured 96% by buy-and-hold doing nothing. The theorem is real. It does not pay this century. Kill two: our flagship committee — the fortress (DBB, EWU, EWZ, GDX, TUR; a different five-asset book from the sigil pentagram drawn above) — was chosen with hindsight. Its five assets' six-year growth rates landed within one point of each other — percentile zero against random baskets. Forward, you cannot make that pick: in worlds with no mean reversion, a random five-asset rebalanced book loses to holding 90% of the time. Kill three: we ran the forty-year-old tests that check whether a mean-reverting relationship persists — 12,216 pre-registered pair-tests, both time directions. Pairs that pass in one era keep reverting in the next era at the false-positive rate of the test itself, and on actual harvest the screen anti-predicts: pairs that looked most cointegrated earned less. We reproduced the published literature (Clegg 2014) on our own universe, exactly.
And even when a real one is found, the market performs the fourth kill for you: the GLD/GDX pair — gold against gold miners, a genuinely tethered relationship — was published by Ernest Chan in his quant-trading book; the follow-up studies that tracked it found it profitable for roughly two more years, then dead of crowding. That is the strange physics of this field: finance is the only discipline where publishing a true finding makes it false. Two filters stand between you and any picked demon — crowding eats everything detectable by standard tools on public data, and drift noise eats everything else, because expected return is the one quantity a decade of data cannot pin down. Notice what survived every filter on this page: the statutes (the tax chapters — nobody can arb your bracket), the insurance rules (they respond to observed damage instead of predicting), and volatility itself (the one quantity in finance that genuinely forecasts its own future). Structure survives. Prediction dies. We committed that sentence to our log in capital letters and it has not lost since.
