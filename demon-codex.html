Lesson one — Shannon's Demon, from zero
Take two assets that each go nowhere long-term but wander violently, and suppose they wander differently — when one is up the other is often down. Hold them 50/50 and rebalance on a schedule: trim whichever rose, buy whichever fell. Each trim sells high; each buy fills low; the discipline manufactures a positive compounding rate out of pure disagreement, even though neither asset earns anything on its own. Claude Shannon demonstrated this on a blackboard decades ago. It is not a paradox and not free money from nothing — it is payment for providing liquidity against your own book's noise. The catch, and the whole research program, is that the textbook version assumes no frictions, no taxes, and no trends. Everything below is about what survives contact with those.
The demon lives in the spread between assets, never in an asset. Excess growth rate of a pair ≈ ½ · var(return spread) · 252 — annualized pairwise demon yield. This one scalar is the heat map the whole apparatus is built around.
Lesson two — the operator, not the rentier
An ETF is congealed multiplicity — a hundred firms in one handle. The rentier re-atomizes it: relates to the bundle as a single object to hold or flee, and reaches for a 200-day moving average as his only defense. The operator keeps it as one node in an ensemble and works the edges between nodes. Composability is the whole game — and it is also a class position. You are not asked to predict anything; you are asked to run a machine whose fuel is the volatility everyone else is fleeing.

Lesson four — the aggression dial Measured on one family
Demons rank by order — how many sleeves sit on the committee (1st order = a pair, 2nd = a triad, and so on). The census taught us what order actually is: the aggression dial. Low order concentrates the engine — feast, offense, fat yield, single points of failure. High order widens the shell — defense, insurance, survival everywhere, thin yield. The price per click is mathematical: equal-weight committees dilute each pairwise edge by 1/k², so every rung up costs roughly 1.5 points of calibrated yield in exchange for width. The seven laws Built & tested
I · Prime the Spring
Rebalance INTO drawdowns
Each buy of a crashing sleeve is a low-basis, high-share lot. The discipline loads the spring under tension; the reversion releases it. Drawdown isn't the enemy — it's the raw material.
II · The Bite
Trim rich → buy poor, $1 units
Sell one executable dollar-unit from the most-over-target sleeve, buy it into the most-under. Human-runnable quanta, no fractional-penny fantasy.
III · The Plateau
Bite size is a drawdown & tax lever, not a returns lever
1%–5% of book all return the same. Size controls drawdown and taxable events — and it's cadence-conditional: every-2-days @ 500bps ≈ daily @ 200bps.
IV · The Free Demon
Contributions deploy dispersion-first
Route each drip to the most-underweight sleeve — never pro-rata, never selling. Free rebalancing, zero taxable events. But only where there's real dispersion; on a flat book the cash waits.
V · The Tax Knob
Widen the band, not the returns
5% → 10% completion band = identical returns, ~4× fewer taxable events. The band is a tax dial.
VI · Demon Routing
Advisory only
Route through the highest spread-variance pair when several qualify. Under resampling it's a coin flip (50.4%). Tiebreaker, never an oracle.
VII · The Gate
OFF
The 200-day trend filter is a passive holder's tool. It cost ~2% of terminal wealth in every window to shave the productive drawdown the harvest is paid to take. Anti-spring. Dropped.
The Tournament of Power — twelve universes Built · ~42M worlds simulated
Bootstrap worlds from one dataset are all children of one reality — the same market physics reshuffled. A demon that survives them has survived one universe's statistics. Real robustness means surviving structurally different physics. So every specimen runs across twelve universe-types, 250 worlds each. A demon that fails a universe is erased from it. The Atlas method — how the judging works The vault is public doctrine
Early on we had a "judge" — a script that ranked demons and kept summary verdicts. We killed it. Its replacement is the Atlas: an append-only vault of raw per-world outcomes. Every cell = one (committee, policy, universe) triple, 250 simulated worlds, every final balance written to disk the moment it exists. Judging is a query, run at read time — change your mind about the metric and you re-read, never re-simulate. Crashes cost nothing (the vault survives; the run resumes). Deepening is additive. The current vault: 168,575 cells across a 97-symbol pool — roughly 42 million simulated worlds. On top of it sit three instruments:
The Compiler
Committee yield compiles from pairs
Fernholz: a committee's harvest is the weighted sum of its edges' spread variances. Calibrated against 861 measured pairs: yield ≈ 0.50·γ* − 0.84·dispersion − 0.02 (R² = 0.93). The dispersion term is the trend tax — buy-and-hold's momentum bonus that raw theory can't see. Screens millions of committees analytically; survival still has to be simulated.
The Hodl Gate
A column, not a filter
Every card records whether the committee's held version survives (median hold ≥ 60% of capital in every universe). Un-hodlable "winners" — vol products, leveraged decay pairs — beat holding only because holding them is a funeral. Zero exceptions found so far; still a law-candidate, not a law.
The Ablation Protocol
Higher orders must earn their order
Every serious committee gets leave-one-out testing against all its subsets and banked edges. If performance survives every ablation except minus-X, the committee was just X-with-bodyguards. The Sculptor runs this backward: start from the whole pool, carve greedily down, record the champion at every order.
Cautionary ward, learned the expensive way: metrics produce artifacts. An inverse-leveraged pair once topped the yield leaderboard at +34%/yr — on a +0.2% CAGR (rebalancing "beat" two instruments grinding to zero). A 3× fund plus its own index topped the CAGR board — a regime trophy, not a strategy. And the beloved forum portfolio HFEA (55/45 UPRO/TMF, rebalanced quarterly)? Sweeping all 99 ratios found the best one was 99/1 — as little TMF as possible. The doctrine's own rebalancing was the murder weapon, faithfully marrying the winner to an asset that fell 89%. Exhaustive search finds coincidences by construction; the tournament and the gates exist to kill them.

Methodology — the epistemics
  • Ledger over theory. The broker's confirmed fills outrank every backtest and every doc, including this one. Reconcile to 6 decimals.
  • Parity gates. Before any comparison, the unchanged code path is re-run and hash-compared byte-for-byte. Every feature ships inert-by-default. The fast vectorized engine is only trusted where it matches the reference engine to tolerance — where it doesn't, the slow engine stays canonical.
  • The tournament as judge. Findings must survive twelve universes, not one leaderboard. In-sample wins are hypotheses, not results.
  • Selection-bias honesty. Exhaustive search over thousands of demons produces winners by construction. Log what was dropped; never quote a top row as doctrine.
  • Append-only results. Raw outcomes are written once and never edited; conclusions are queries over the vault. Anyone can re-ask a different question of the same data.
  • The primitive stack. Stdlib Python, numpy where parity-gated, vanilla HTML/JS. Libraries, not frameworks. Don't hold what everyone can farm.
