The sweep, by the numbers
① Top CAGR — best row per pair
The entire top of the CAGR board is the same shape: 90% leveraged Nasdaq engine plus a 10% sidekick, riding one of the best Nasdaq/semiconductor windows in history. The winner, TQQQ/SMH 90/10 with a 4% threshold rebalance, turned $1,000 into ~$8,360 (CAGR ≈ 84.7%) — while spending part of the ride down ≈ 56%.
⚠ Regime trophy, not strategy validation. A 3x leveraged fund plus a highly correlated partner (corr ≈ 0.89) in a monster bull window. The −56% max drawdown is the price of admission, and the next window is under no obligation to rhyme.
② Spotlight — GLDM/TQQQ, the "gold multiplies TQQQ" story, stated precisely
Gold does pair beautifully with TQQQ in this window — but the best configuration is 90% TQQQ engine with GLDM as a 10% ballast. Almost all the return is TQQQ itself; the "multiplication" is regime co-movement (gold and the Nasdaq both ripped), not rebalance alpha. Demon yield — annualized rebalance alpha vs the same-weight buy-and-hold — is slightly negative for every rebalanced GLDM/TQQQ row here (≈ −0.03% to −0.31%/yr). What rebalancing did buy: a shallower max drawdown (−53.5% vs −56.3%) and a slightly better Sharpe (1.37 vs 1.33).
Verdict: gold acted as ballast / stabilizer, not as rebalance-alpha proof. Say "gold stabilized TQQQ here," not "gold multiplied TQQQ."
③ Top demon yield — the YANG/YINN fine print
The demon-yield and excess-vs-best-single-leg boards are dominated by YANG/YINN, the 3x China bear/bull inverse pair (corr ≈ −0.998). The best row "earns" +34.3%/yr of demon yield… on an absolute CAGR of +0.2% ($1,000 → ~$1,008 in 3.5 years). Every other top YANG/YINN row is negative outright. That's textbook volatility harvesting on two instruments that grind toward zero: rebalancing "beats" holding either leg while the absolute result stays flat or worse. The one non-inverse pair that cracks the excess board (GME/XLE) is included for contrast — positive demon yield and positive absolute return.
⚠ Demon yield can be a metric artifact — profit is not implied. YANG/YINN is filed as an inverse-pair / volatility-harvesting curiosity worth stress testing (other windows, realistic costs, decay mechanics), not a strategy.
④ Best row per family — so one hot pair doesn't flood the view
Each pair was tagged with a rough structural family, named role + role from a small fixed vocabulary: leveraged engine and engine for the growth legs (a second engine gets tagged correlated or idiosyncratic), global diversifier and commodity beta for the in-betweens, and gold / cash / defensive ballast for the stabilizers — plus one special family for the YANG/YINN inverse pair. These are demon family names — research labels for sorting a 7,600-row sweep, not doctrine roles: tagging SGOV a cash ballast classifies a simulated pair, it doesn't assign anything a job in a real book. Showing the best row per family keeps TQQQ-and-friends from occupying every slot, and makes the middle of the distribution visible: plain engines, defensive mixes, and ballast-on-ballast pairs that merely doubled.
Caveats — carried from the sweep, abbreviated but not optional
  • Simulated research only. Hypothetical backtest rows — not doctrine, not live-trading evidence, not observed real-money performance, not investment advice.
  • Price-return only. Dividends are not modeled, which understates income-heavy legs (SGOV / TLT / XLP types).
  • Selection bias / multiple comparisons by construction. 7,600 rows of exhaustive search means the top rows are selection-biased; a 190-pair sweep will always produce a shiny leaderboard.
  • One window. A single ~3.5-year window (2023-01-03 → 2026-06-29) whose regime strongly favored gold and the Nasdaq.
  • Partial rebalance cost estimates; taxes not modeled; split adjustment unverified outside the verified core symbols.
  • dollar_first_1usd carries a doctrine label for comparison only — it is not an execution path.
